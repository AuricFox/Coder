Directed Graph

A directed graph, also known as a digraph, is similar to an undirected graph, but with one key distinction: edges have a direction associated with them. This means that the relationship between vertices is asymmetrical; an edge connecting vertex A to vertex B does not necessarily imply a connection from B to A.

Here are the main components and characteristics of directed graphs:

Vertices (Nodes): These are the fundamental units of the graph. Each vertex typically represents some entity or object, and they are connected by edges.

Edges: Edges in a directed graph have a direction associated with them. An edge from vertex A to vertex B indicates a directed connection from A to B. This means you can traverse the edge only in the specified direction.

Adjacency: In directed graphs, adjacency is directional. If there is an edge from vertex A to vertex B, vertex A is said to be adjacent to vertex B, but not necessarily vice versa. This is a crucial distinction from undirected graphs.

In-degree and Out-degree: In-degree of a vertex in a directed graph is the number of edges directed into that vertex, while out-degree is the number of edges directed out from that vertex.

Connectivity: Connectivity in directed graphs refers to the existence of directed paths between vertices. A directed graph may have different types of connectivity properties compared to undirected graphs due to the directional nature of the edges.

Directed graphs are widely used in various domains such as computer science (e.g., modeling internet traffic, representing dependencies in software systems), social network analysis (e.g., modeling follower relationships in social media), transportation networks (e.g., representing one-way streets in a city), and more. They provide a powerful tool for representing and analyzing relationships with directional dependencies between entities.
