Weighted Graph

A weighted graph is a type of graph in which each edge has an associated numerical value, called a weight. These weights represent some measure of distance, cost, capacity, or any other relevant metric associated with traversing that edge.

Here are the main points about weighted graphs:

Vertices (Nodes): These are the fundamental units of the graph. Each vertex typically represents some entity or object, and they are connected by edges.

Edges: Edges in a weighted graph not only connect vertices but also have a weight associated with them. This weight can represent various attributes depending on the context of the problem being modeled. For example, in a transportation network, the weight could represent the distance between two locations, in a communication network, it could represent latency, and in a social network, it could represent the strength of relationship between individuals.

Weights: The weights associated with edges are typically non-negative numbers, although they can be negative or even non-numeric in some specialized cases. The interpretation of the weights depends on the specific application of the graph.

Path Weight: The weight of a path in a weighted graph is the sum of the weights of all the edges along that path. Finding paths with minimum or maximum total weight is a common task in many algorithms and applications involving weighted graphs.

Weighted graphs provide a flexible and powerful framework for modeling and analyzing relationships between entities where the edges have associated numerical values that represent some meaningful information about the connections between vertices. Weighted graphs are used in various real-world scenarios such as route planning (finding the shortest or fastest path between two locations), network optimization (minimizing costs or maximizing efficiency in communication or transportation networks), resource allocation (determining the optimal distribution of resources based on costs or capacities), and more.
