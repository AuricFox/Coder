Undirected Graph

An undirected graph is a mathematical structure that consists of a collection of vertices (also called nodes) and edges connecting those vertices. In an undirected graph, edges have no direction associated with them; they simply connect two vertices. This means that if there is an edge connecting vertex A to vertex B, you can traverse it from A to B or from B to A interchangeably.

Here are some key points about undirected graphs:

Vertices (Nodes): These are the fundamental units of the graph. Each vertex typically represents some entity or object, and they are connected by edges.

Edges: These are the connections between vertices. In an undirected graph, an edge simply denotes a relationship or connection between two vertices. Edges are usually represented by lines connecting the vertices.

Adjacency: Two vertices are said to be adjacent if there is an edge connecting them. In an undirected graph, adjacency is symmetric, meaning if vertex A is adjacent to vertex B, then vertex B is also adjacent to vertex A.

Degree: The degree of a vertex in an undirected graph is the number of edges incident to it, i.e., the number of edges connected to that vertex.

Connectivity: In undirected graphs, connectivity refers to the ability to traverse from one vertex to another via edges. If there exists a path between any two vertices in the graph, the graph is said to be connected.

Undirected graphs find applications in various fields such as computer science, social network analysis, transportation networks, and more. They serve as a fundamental model for representing relationships and connections between entities in a wide range of systems.
